The Patterns

This week: sincere, scripture-quoting, often moving — and almost entirely governed by what the listener was sent away to do.

The Temperature

This was a storm week, and not a close one. Every sermon scored landed in the lowest band, with the spread running narrow and low. The striking thing is not that any single message was careless — several were thoughtful, well-built, and warm. The striking thing is how uniformly the weight of each message rested on the listener rather than on the finished work of Christ. When ten different pulpits drift the same direction in the same week, the current is worth naming.

The Governing Pattern

The dominant move was structural, not verbal. Most of these sermons mentioned the cross. Several stated it plainly and well. But mention is not government. In message after message, the cross arrived late — as the third illustration's payload, as a closing prayer bolted onto a message that had already done its real work, as the salvation invitation after the body had been spent on something else. The question a sermon answers in its final five minutes is the question it was actually asking all along, and this week that question was almost always “what will you now do?”

Warm but Ungrounded

The week's most common shape was a behavioral or responsive message with a grace ornament. Identity was frequently affirmed — listeners were told who they are in striking, generous language — but the affirmation functioned as a runway. You are a child of God, so now go all in. You are loved, so now make space, position yourself, don't miss the moment. Identity used as setup for assignment is not the same as identity grounded in a finished work, and the rubric registers the difference sharply.

One Sermon, Made Concrete

To make the pattern tangible without identifying anyone: one message exposited its passage competently, even naming that the law revealed humanity's need for a savior and that the new covenant rests on what Jesus did rather than on earning. Strong words. But the entire sermon was then organized around responding to the moment — eyes up, go all in, make space for God to move — and it closed by asking whether the listener would respond. The covenant language was real. It simply never governed.

Where the Foundation Disappears

The weakest readings clustered exactly where you would expect when response governs. The category measuring whether transformation flows from who you already are in Christ — rather than from trying harder — was the lowest on the board. Close behind was the measure of whether the message handed the listener a finished gift or an assignment. When change is sourced in effort, the gospel quietly becomes advice. Several of the week's messages were occasion-driven — a Father's Day theme, a summer series — and the genre pulled hard toward exhortation, which is part of why the storm was so widespread.

A sermon can quote the cross and still be carried by the listener's response — and when it is, the listener walks out with a task, not a rest.

The Editorial

Here is the New Covenant point underneath all of this. The finished work of Christ is not the introduction to the Christian life that we then leave behind for the real business of doing. It is the ground we never step off of. A message that affirms identity beautifully and then spends its energy on what to do with that identity has, however unintentionally, told the listener that the foundation was the easy part and the response is where the weight lives. The apostolic pattern runs the other way — gift first, and the gift so complete that the response flows from rest rather than toward it. A storm week is not a verdict on anyone's sincerity or calling. It is a reading of one current, and this week that current ran away from the cross even in rooms that named it.

The Index

The Grace Clarity Index (GCI) measures how clearly a sermon articulates the finished work of Christ as the foundation of the believer's identity, security, and righteousness — and whether the apostolic indicative-imperative pattern (gift first, then response) is honored when fruit and calling come up. It does not evaluate sincerity or salvation. Each sermon is scored across 13 categories totaling 100 points. Grades: A 90–100, B 80–89, C 65–79, D 50–64, F 0–49. Two hard rules govern the ceiling: if the finished work is not clearly the foundation, the score is capped (the Ceiling Rule); if the believer's full righteousness before God because of Christ alone is never clearly stated, it is capped lower still (the Core Lock).

The Index reads every sermon through one lens, and it holds that lens openly. It asks whether the believer's standing was grounded in what Christ finished, or in what the listener was sent away to do. That is a New Covenant standard, and it is not neutral — a preacher from another tradition would not so much fail this index as decline its premise. So a low reading is a diagnosis, not a verdict. It does not measure sincerity, gifting, or whether a life was changed in that room. It measures one current running through the week's teaching, and nothing more. Calibration check: the Index is periodically run against preachers known for explicit finished-work teaching to confirm it can register clear skies. It can.
