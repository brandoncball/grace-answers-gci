The Full Measure

A zero in the A-range is uncommon. This week, it happened. Not one of America's largest and most widely watched pulpits articulated the completed nature of Christ's redemption with enough clarity to shape the whole sermon. Some came close. Two sermons rose to the B-range by grounding identity explicitly in the finished work—declaring "It is finished" and building from there. But the pattern across the group was unmistakable: the sermon became about the believer's response rather than Christ's completed action.

The Identity Trap

Identity language showed up frequently, but almost always as a runway to activation. The formula was recognizable: "You are complete in Christ" followed immediately by "now go cultivate," "now carry," "now change your angle." Identity became the theological permission slip for effort. The distinction matters. Identity rooted in finished work says: Because He is done, you are secure. Identity-as-setup says: Now that you understand who you are, perform. Half the sermons in this week's set fell into the second camp.

"Grace doesn't last forever. Obedience is the condition for blessing."
Easter and the Activation Squeeze

Several sermons leaned into Easter theology—strong cross language, explicit atonement teaching, language around the tomb and resurrection. All good. But in nearly every case, the final 5 minutes shifted hard into activation mode. The theology held until the closing prayer, where surrender, change, and cultivation language dominated. The scores dropped not because the doctrine was wrong, but because the final pitch said: The work is done—now you must do yours. One sermon explicitly stated that grace itself has expiration date—obedience is the condition for blessing's continuation. That one landed in the F range, and rightly so.

The Weakest Categories

Two categories bottomed out this week: Source of Transformation (5.0/8) and Believer Identity (5.0/7). Both came down to the same issue: change was framed as something the listener must generate rather than something God generates on the basis of union with Christ. When sermons landed well on these categories, they spoke of the Spirit's work, rest, and the believer's participation in Christ's finished work. When they landed poorly, they handed the listener a to-do list and called it grace.

Assurance—Affirmed but Homeless

Assurance and Security (7.8/10) was the strongest category this week. Most sermons affirmed God's love, God's faithfulness, God's presence. But here's the catch: that assurance rarely hung on the finished work. It hung on God's character, God's promises, God's consistency. Those are true. But when assurance has no foundation in "It is finished," it becomes another thing God is doing for you—not a resting place you can never lose because it's grounded in something Christ already completed.

The Distribution Tells the Story

Two B-range sermons, two C-range, one D, one F. The spread (37 to 88) is enormous—it reflects the vast theological distance between America's biggest pulpits. Some have grasped grace. Others are still handing the believer the ball. The absence of an A is the week's real headline.

The Index — How This Works

The Grace Clarity Index scores sermons across 12 categories designed to measure whether the finished work of Christ is doing the theological work in the message. The index asks: Is identity rooted in Christ's completed redemption, or is it set up as a runway to what you must do? Is assurance grounded in "It is finished," or in what you must maintain? Is transformation portrayed as the Spirit's work in union with Christ, or as the result of your effort and response?

Each sermon receives a composite score (0–100), placed on a weather scale. The scale reflects not how good the preaching is, but whether the sermon's theological center holds to New Covenant clarity. Public report includes aggregate data only.
