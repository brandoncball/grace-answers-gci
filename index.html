The Patterns

Visibility

Visibility came in at 49 — partly cloudy. Not a storm, not a clearing: a sky with real light in it and a layer of cloud over the sun. Across 9 sermons, not one rose above partly cloudy, and two slipped into overcast. The grace was audible; it just wasn’t load-bearing.

The Governing Pattern

Here is the shape that repeated almost everywhere this week: a sermon states something genuinely true about the finished work — you are complete, you are forgiven, the cross settled it — and then pivots, and the pivot is where the weight goes. The declaration opens the message; a listener-move closes it. In the language of the Index, Proclamation outran Governance in every sermon scored. Grace was preached this week, and it was not what the sermons ran on.

Warm but Ungrounded

A sermon that opened by naming a real gospel moment then spent its middle and its landing on what the listener must now do — surrender, activate, step out, pick it up. The turn, not the declaration, carried the close. The finished work was the doorway, not the floor.

The Bright Spot

The strongest readings this week each carried a genuinely moving gospel climax — a kinsman-redeemer, a cross-centered appeal, a real invitation to believe. What kept them under mostly clear was not a missing sentence but a missing spine: the grace was stated and then the structure turned elsewhere. That is a description of the instrument’s one question, not a criticism of the preaching.

A Note on the New Instrument

This is the first Forecast published on a rebuilt Index (v3.0). The old version stacked penalties; the new one simply asks whether the finished work earns the message’s structure, and awards points where it does. Most numbers moved — and mostly up — for an honest reason: we stopped fining sermons and started measuring them. The bands are new, too. Partly cloudy now means grace was clearly present but response governed the message; overcast means the gospel sat in the background. A higher number than last season does not mean a message improved; it means the ruler changed. Read the band, not the delta.

Grace was preached this week. It simply was not what the sermons ran on.

The Editorial

The New Covenant does not ask the believer to activate what Christ finished; it asks the believer to rest in it. A message can be warm and orthodox in its sentences and still send people home to carry a weight Jesus already carried. That is the gap the Index keeps finding — not heresy, but a center of gravity that keeps sliding from what He did to what we must do. When the covenant is kept clear, the sermon lands on rest. This week, most landed on movement.

The Index

The Grace Clarity Index (GCI) measures how clearly a sermon presents the finished work of Christ as the foundation of the believer’s identity, security, and righteousness. Each sermon is scored on ten items across three dimensions: what it proclaims, what governs its structure, and how it handles the text and the room. The composite is simple arithmetic: two times the sum of the ten items. It does not evaluate sincerity or salvation.
The Index reads every sermon through one lens, and it holds that lens openly. It asks whether the believer’s standing was grounded in what Christ finished, or in what the listener was sent away to do. That is a New Covenant standard, and it is not neutral. A preacher from another tradition would not so much fail this index as decline its premise. So a low reading is a diagnosis, not a verdict. It does not measure sincerity, gifting, or whether a life was changed in that room. It measures one current running through the week’s teaching, and nothing more.
Calibration check: the Index is periodically run against preachers known for explicit finished-work teaching to confirm it can register clear skies. It can.
