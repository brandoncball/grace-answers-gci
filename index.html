This week: sincere, warm, often moving — but across the board the believer's effort, response, and experience carried the weight the finished work was meant to carry.

The Patterns

The Temperature

Nine sermons, and the storm band held. The average rose to 26 — the highest reading in three weeks, lifted partly by a Pentecost-weekend turn toward the Spirit and the risen Christ — but it is still a storm reading. Eight of the nine landed in the F band; one cleared into the overcast. The warmth was real. The clarity was not.

The Governing Pattern

The recurring move was not a denial of grace but a quiet substitution. Sermon after sermon opened a door to the finished work, then handed the listener the load: be diligent, make the ask, measure up, pray longer, bring your pain, embrace the change. The cross showed up — often beautifully — but rarely as the thing actually doing the work.

Warm but Ungrounded

Where assurance appeared, it was most often anchored in God's faithfulness or felt presence rather than in an accomplished righteousness. That is a tender, true note. But nearness without explanation is sentimentality, not theology — and a listener left knowing God is close, without knowing why they stand accepted, is left to manufacture the standing themselves.

One Sermon, In Miniature

One sermon declared the believer complete and remembered by God, then organized its whole second half around what the listener must keep doing so the relationship doesn't fade. The identity was stated and then immediately put back on the clock. That is the week in a single motion: grace announced, then revoked as a wage.

The Bright Spot

One message broke from the pack by keeping Christ at the center the whole way through — a road-to-Emmaus reflection that refused to make the point about the listener's outcome and kept returning to the risen Jesus who comes close. It is honored here for getting nearer than any other this week. It stayed in the overcast rather than clearing only because its ground was Christ's presence rather than His explicitly stated, finished righteousness — the standard the index calls the Core Lock (the requirement that the believer's full acceptance in Christ alone be plainly said, not implied).

Where the Covenant Disappears

The old-covenant/new-covenant distinction was the quietest category on the board again. Few messages told the listener which covenant they are standing in, which is precisely why so many drifted, by gravity, toward do this and God will respond.

When the finished work is admired but not trusted to carry the message, grace becomes the decoration on a sermon about the self.

The Editorial

None of this is a question of sincerity, and it is not an accusation. It is a measurement of where the weight sits. The New Covenant does not ask the believer to sustain a standing Christ already secured; it announces a finished righteousness and lets the response flow from rest. A week that rose four points and still sits in the storm is not a failure of warmth. It is an invitation back to the one foundation that was never ours to hold up.

How this works. The Grace Clarity Index measures how clearly a sermon articulates the finished work of Christ as the foundation of the believer's identity, security, and righteousness — and whether the apostolic pattern of gift-first-then-response is honored when fruit and calling come up. It does not evaluate sincerity or salvation. Each sermon is scored across 13 categories totaling 100 points. Grades: A 90–100 · B 80–89 · C 65–79 · D 50–64 · F 0–49. Two hard rules cap the score: the Ceiling Rule (if the finished work isn't clearly the foundation, the score cannot exceed 70) and the Core Lock Rule (if the believer's full righteousness in Christ alone isn't clearly stated, the score cannot exceed 60).
