The Temperature

The aggregate landed at 13/100, with the watchlist's first non-F sermon in two weeks pulling the top end up to 58 — a C, in the Mixed-Grace-but-Diluted band. Not absent in language across the watchlist — sermons mentioned the cross, named Jesus, prayed warmly — but in ten of eleven, the finished work did not function as the foundation of the message.

The Governing Pattern

The diagnostic question isn't "Was Jesus mentioned?" It's "What was doing the theological work?" This week, in ten of eleven scored sermons, the answer was the listener's response. The believer was on the clock. One sermon framed the entire week's spiritual outlook as a function of personal alignment. Another asked the listener to "position yourself" for a breakthrough. Another reframed an Old Testament command to rebuild a temple as a present-tense imperative for marriages today. The cross showed up as decoration, not as foundation.

Warm Words, Ungrounded

The most striking pattern: sermons that used warm, encouraging, even affectionate language while leaving the finished work entirely outside the message's load-bearing structure. Nearness without explanation is sentimentality, not theology. A listener could leave several of these sermons feeling moved without ever encountering a sentence stating that they are fully accepted by God because of what Christ already accomplished. The gospel was implied, gestured at, sometimes tacked on as a closing prayer — but never functioning as the spine.

Where the Covenant Disappears

Old Testament texts handed to New Covenant believers without translation. A passage about rebuilding a temple becoming a marriage-renewal blueprint. A pre-cross encounter with a religious self-justifier turned into a Christian behavioral test. A pre-resurrection wisdom text mined for present-day life optimization. The Old-vs-New Covenant rollup landed at 2.0 / 10 across the watchlist — the lowest of the four headline metrics. If everything is for you, nothing is finished.

The Bright Spot

The week's highest scorer (58/100, C — Mixed Grace) honored honestly. It carried a strong image of just judgment falling on Christ rather than the believer — a clean substitutionary moment in the body of the message. What kept it from B was the absence of a clear union-with-Christ frame; the gospel was preached at the listener instead of grounded in the listener's already-completed standing. That gap is what the Core Lock standard — a threshold requiring the foundational claim of the gospel to be explicitly stated — is built to catch. A sermon that gets close enough to land in C is a marker the index can work with.

Methodology Note

The Index's scoring rubric was refined this week. Deduction stacking is now capped at −25 (previously uncompounded), and the floor is now 5 (previously 0), with 0 reserved for sermons that explicitly contradict the gospel. The band boundaries were also rebalanced so the four upper grades (A, B, C, D) cover equal 15-point widths instead of compressing toward the top. These changes preserve diagnostic distance between sermons clustered near the floor without shifting where any given sermon actually lives. Under the previous rubric this week's average was 9; under the refined rubric it is 13. The story the data tells has not changed.

When the cross is decoration and the listener is the driver, even a sincere sermon ends up asking the audience to finish what only Christ could finish.

The Editorial

The point of a sermon isn't to bring people up to a standard. It's to declare that the standard has already been met for them. When that order gets reversed — when Christian living gets named before Christ's accomplished work gets named — the listener leaves with a weight they were never given to carry. This week's data is what that reversal looks like when it shows up across an entire weekend of widely-watched pulpits. Not absent in language. Absent as the governing foundation.

The Index — How This Works

The Grace Clarity Index (GCI) measures how clearly a sermon articulates the finished work of Christ as the foundation of the believer's identity, security, and righteousness — and whether the apostolic indicative-imperative pattern is honored when fruit and calling come up. It does not evaluate sincerity or salvation. Each sermon is scored across 13 categories totaling 100 points. Grades: A 86–100, B 71–85, C 56–70, D 41–55, F 5–40. Two hard rules cap the result: the Ceiling Rule limits any sermon whose foundation is not the finished work to a maximum of 70, and the Core Lock Rule limits any sermon that does not explicitly state full righteousness in Christ alone to a maximum of 60.
