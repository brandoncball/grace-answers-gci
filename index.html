The Patterns

The Temperature

Nine sermons scored this week, and an average of 56 places the week firmly in "Overcast" territory. The preaching this week wasn't hostile to grace — it simply wasn't built on it. Most messages mentioned Jesus, referenced the cross, or acknowledged God's love. But in sermon after sermon, the theological weight shifted from what Christ accomplished to what the listener needed to do next. The warmth was genuine. The theology wasn't.

The Governing Pattern

What's doing the theological work this week: the believer's response. Seven of nine sermons landed on activation — a commitment to stay faithful, a choice to love harder, a fasting practice to adopt, a ministry to engage. Only one sermon let the listener leave resting. The believer was on the clock in nearly every message this week. This isn't accidental. It's architectural. When the finished work isn't the foundation, the believer's effort becomes the only engine available.

Warm but Ungrounded

Several sermons this week opened with genuine pastoral warmth and even strong theological language. One message declared that God's wrath was satisfied and no favor needed earning — then immediately pivoted to a discipline as the mechanism for spiritual breakthrough. Another built a beautiful case for the soul being loved by God, grounding assurance in divine omniscience rather than human performance. These moments of clarity prove the theology is accessible. The question is whether it will be allowed to govern.

A concrete example: A sermon that declared believers are already at peace with God through Christ immediately followed with a series of imperatives — choose to love, choose to forgive, become distributors of peace. The finished work became the runway, not the destination. Identity shifted into assignment. The soul wasn't sent into the week resting on its union with Christ; it was sent with a job description.

The Bright Spot

One sermon this week scored in the B range, anchoring identity in God's love for the soul and grounding assurance in divine foreknowledge. Where most sermons this week made the believer the engine, this one made God's love the engine and positioned the believer as the recipient. What kept it from an A: distance language suggesting the soul still needs to "draw near" undermined the New Covenant reality of permanent union. But the direction was clear. The foundation was Christ. The rest followed.

Where the Covenant Disappears

The weakest category this week: Law and Covenant Clarity, averaging just 46% of its possible score. Most sermons operated without any framework for distinguishing old and new covenant. Pre-cross teaching was applied directly to believers without acknowledging the cross changed everything. One sermon used the Lord's Prayer as a binding template for Christian prayer without noting it was spoken under the old covenant to people who had not yet received the Spirit. When the covenant distinction disappears, everything becomes law. And when everything is law, grace becomes decoration.

"Most pulpits this week mentioned the finished work. Almost none were built on it."

The Editorial

The data this week tells a consistent story: America's largest pulpits are pastorally warm and scripturally engaged, but they are handing listeners a job description instead of a birth certificate. The average score of 56 means the typical sermon this week acknowledged Christ but governed on the believer's response. The finished work appeared in opening statements and never returned. The closing prayers were calls to action, not invitations to rest. What this measures is that the gospel was mentioned but not preached. Seven of nine sermons landed diagnostics registered "trying harder." This is not an accusation — it is a measurement. It is also reversible. The theology is there. It simply needs to be allowed to drive.

The Index

The Grace Clarity Index (GCI) measures how clearly a sermon articulates the finished work of Christ as the foundation of the believer's identity, security, and righteousness. It does not evaluate sincerity, salvation, or spiritual fruit. Each sermon is scored across 12 categories totaling 100 points.

Letter Grade Scale:
A (90–100): Exceptional. The finished work is the foundation and the governor.
B (80–89): Strong. Clear New Covenant foundation with minor inconsistencies.
C (65–79): Adequate. Mentions grace but drifts toward performance as the mechanism.
D (50–64): Weak. More emphasis on the believer's response than Christ's accomplishment.
F (0–49): Poor. Works-oriented framework; law-governed; finished work absent or contradicted.
Two Hard Rules:
Rule 1: A sermon cannot score above 65 without distinguishing old and new covenant.
Rule 2: A sermon cannot score above 70 if the believer leaves the message with an assignment rather than assurance.
