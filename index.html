The Patterns — This Week's Observations

The Temperature

Nine sermons. Zero above a C. An average of 44 — firmly in Storm Warning territory. This isn't a week where the gospel was preached badly. In most cases, it was barely preached at all as a theological claim about what Christ accomplished. Preachers were sincere. The preaching was often warm, emotionally skilled, and culturally intelligent. But warmth is not the same as finished-work clarity, and this week was a precise illustration of that difference.

The Governing Pattern

Across nearly every sermon this week, the theological work was being done by the listener's response, not by Christ's completed act. Sermons asked listeners to seek Jesus harder, cultivate the Spirit's welcome, fix their minds on peace, position themselves for breakthrough, or sign up to serve. One message — built entirely around what five human experiences produce faith-growth — did not engage the finished work of Christ in any form. The believer's posture was doing the heavy lifting in every case except two. The finished work, when it appeared at all, functioned as background music rather than the score.

Warm but Ungrounded

Several sermons this week preached genuine grace content — God's compassion doesn't fail, God loves unconditionally, you are accepted. That's close. But close is not the same as landed. When a sermon says "God still cares even when you've failed" without anchoring that care in what Christ did to absorb the judgment your failure deserved, the assurance it offers is one hard week away from feeling untrue. Therapeutic grace — the feeling of being loved — can be felt and lost. Finished-work grace — the fact of being righteous in God's sight because of Christ's record — cannot be unfelt, because it isn't a feeling. It's a fact. This week's warmest sermons knew the feeling; they didn't find the fact.

The Concrete Pattern

A sermon that declared "you are complete in Christ" immediately followed with "so fix your gaze on him, lean in, re-engage" — shifting completeness into an ongoing maintenance task. If you're already complete, there's nothing to maintain. The sermon got to the right destination in one sentence and then drove away from it for twenty minutes.

The Bright Spot

Two sermons scored in the upper D range and warrant genuine recognition. The highest-scoring message was built on Luke 15 — the Prodigal Son — and preached the grace narrative faithfully: the father runs before the son arrives, the robe is given before the behavior changes, the party starts before anything has been proven. "When I was faithless, he was still faithful" was the single strongest finished-work statement of the week. The second highest-scoring message was expository, Christ-centered, and handled the authority-of-Christ theme without burdening the listener with a performance system. Neither sermon triggered the Core Lock rule cleanly — the explicit language of the believer's righteousness-as-gift from Christ alone was not present — and that gap is what keeps both scores in the D range rather than the C or B. You can preach a grace-shaped story without preaching the grace-grounding claim. The prodigal gets the robe, but does the sermon name what the robe costs the Father?

Warmth is not the same as finished-work clarity. You can make people feel loved without telling them why the love is permanent.

The Editorial

Forty-four out of a hundred. The law and covenant clarity category — which measures whether preachers distinguish between what the old covenant demanded and what the new covenant delivers — averaged 3.6 out of 10. That is the diagnostic gap. When preachers don't know the difference between preaching from the old covenant and preaching from the new, they default to Moses with better lighting. The cross becomes inspiration rather than transaction. Grace becomes God's personality trait rather than God's accomplished act. And the listener leaves with a warmer feeling about God but no more certain of their standing before him. The finished work asks one question of every sermon: did the cross change something legally, permanently, and completely on behalf of the sinner? This week's data says most pulpits aren't answering that question. They're answering a different one — how can people feel closer to God? — and calling it gospel.

The Index — How This Works
The Grace Clarity Index (GCI) measures how clearly a sermon articulates the finished work of Christ as the foundation of the believer's identity, security, and righteousness. It does not evaluate sincerity, salvation, or spiritual fruit. Each sermon is scored across 12 categories totaling 100 points. Grades: A (90–100), B (80–89), C (65–79), D (50–64), F (0–49). Two hard rules apply: the Ceiling Rule (finished work not clearly the foundation → max 70) and the Core Lock Rule (believer's righteousness through Christ alone not stated → max 60).
