The Patterns — This Week's Observations

This week: a wider sample, the same diagnosis. Twelve sermons, an average of 31, and the finished work of Christ rarely doing the theological work.

The Temperature

The week again landed deep in the Storm Warning band on the Grace Clarity Index. Of the twelve sermons scored, eleven fell into the F range and one inched into the D range. The pulpits this week were articulate, energetic, and often emotionally compelling — but in nearly every case, the cross was something the message referenced rather than something the message rested on.

The Governing Pattern

The Governing Diagnostic kept producing the same answer: the believer's response. Across the watchlist, messages were organized around what the listener should next say, declare, release, surrender, refocus, position, or do. Where grace appeared, it usually appeared late, in the closing prayer or the altar call, after the body of the message had already done its work through activation language. When the cross showed up earlier, it was almost always serving as motivation for action rather than as the foundation that made the action unnecessary.

Identity as Setup, Not Foundation

The most common pattern this week was a particular trap the index flags by name: identity-as-setup. A sermon will state something true and grounding — you are loved, you are accepted, you are complete in Christ — and then immediately convert that statement into a runway: so refocus, so realign, so step into it, so go first. The strong identity line is not the foundation of the message; it is the cue that the assignment is coming. By the time the closing prayer arrives, the listener has been handed back the work.

One Concrete Example

One sermon this week declared, almost beautifully, that "if we have received Jesus, he has become our completeness, our peace, and he fills every heart." Within a single breath the same prayer continued: "sometimes we stray because we turn our attention elsewhere — let's refocus today, let's lean in, let's re-engage." The theology of the first sentence collapses into the assignment of the second. Identity gets converted into instruction in real time. Soft response language masked as encouragement is still response-driven theology.

When a sermon names what is true about the believer and then immediately tells the believer to activate it, the truth becomes scaffolding for an instruction the cross was meant to make unnecessary.

The Bright Spot

One sermon broke into the D band with a structurally different approach: a verse-by-verse walk through one of Paul's letters that named Christ as the substance of the Old Testament shadow and exhorted listeners to keep walking in him "by simple faith." That sermon scored higher because the body of the message stayed close to the text and to Christ — not because the closing was dramatic. The fix that would lift it into the C range is not a bigger altar call; it is one explicit sentence stating that the believer's full righteousness before God comes through Christ alone, with no contribution required.

Where the Covenant Disappears

The single weakest category this week was Old vs. New Covenant Clarity — an average of 2.1 out of 10 across the cohort. None of the sermons explicitly distinguished the law's role of exposing sin from the new covenant's reality of accomplished righteousness. One closing actually condensed the entire message into the Old Covenant formula: fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the duty of all mankind, with the cross appearing only as motivation to do the keeping. The believer was on the clock again all week.

The Editorial

An average of 31 across twelve sermons, with eleven of them in the F band, is not a fluke or a single bad week. It is the diagnostic working as designed across a much wider sample than usual. The Grace Clarity Index does not measure sincerity, communication skill, or kindness — the pulpits this week were strong on all three. It measures whether the finished work of Christ is the explicit, governing foundation of the message from beginning to end. This week, across a dozen of the most widely heard English-language pulpits in the world, that foundation was largely missing. The warmth was real. The framework asks whether the warmth has anything underneath it.

The Index — How This Works

The Grace Clarity Index (GCI) measures how clearly a sermon articulates the finished work of Christ as the foundation of the believer's identity, security, and righteousness. It does not evaluate sincerity, salvation, or spiritual fruit. Each sermon is scored across 12 categories totaling 100 points.

A 90–100 B 80–89 C 65–79 D 50–64 F 0–49
Ceiling Rule: If the finished work of Christ is not the clear foundation of the message, the score caps at 70.
Core Lock Rule: If the believer's full righteousness before God because of Christ alone is not clearly stated, the score caps at 60.
