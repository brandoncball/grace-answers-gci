This week: Pastoral warmth, sincere intent, but every sermon hands the listener an assignment instead of a finished gift.

The Temperature

This is the lowest weekly average in over a month. Six of the seven sermons landed in the F band. The one that crossed into the D band did so by tying its closing — but only its closing — to the cross. Across the watchlist, what was preached was real, kind, and culturally fluent; what was missing, almost without exception, was a settled foundation in what Christ has already accomplished.

The Governing Pattern

In nearly every sermon this week, the theological work was being done by the listener's response. Take the next step. Pick the next chapter. Practice stillness. Magnify the Lord. Become an ambassador. Order your priorities. The verbs governed; the indicatives of finished work hovered at the margins. This is the difference between a message that sounds biblical and one that is actually grounded in the gospel.

One Concrete Example

One sermon this week told its listeners — explicitly — that they "might not make it" if they did not move forward in the right way. The same sermon mentioned the cross in a single sentence and immediately pivoted: "It's not what's in his hand, it's what's in yours that's going to make the difference." That single move captures the pattern of the whole watchlist. The cross becomes setup; the believer's response becomes the punchline. The cross becomes a footnote to your effort instead of the foundation of your standing.

Where the Covenant Disappears

The category that scored lowest this week was Old vs. New Covenant Clarity — averaging 2 out of 10 across the watchlist. When the covenant is blurred, the message inevitably shifts toward "do this and God will respond." Old Testament narratives — the Red Sea, David's restoration, Samson's last act — were used as direct templates for Christian living, with no covenant lens to filter them. The result is moral instruction wearing gospel clothes.

The Bright Spot

The one sermon that crossed into the D band did one thing the others did not: it said the cross out loud and tied the believer's standing to it — "Jesus died on the cross for my sin and was raised from the grave so that I can be saved." That is a substitution sentence, and it pulled the score up by twenty points. What kept it from a C is that the gospel content lived almost entirely in the closing call to faith. The body of the sermon was therapeutic and motivational; the gospel was the bow at the end, not the spine running through.

When the cross is the closing pitch instead of the foundation, the listener leaves with assignments — not with rest.

The Editorial

This week shows what New Covenant teaching looks like when the new covenant is removed. The sermons were not heretical. They were not unkind. Several of them contained genuinely sharp cultural diagnosis. What they lacked was a foundation. The believer's full righteousness before God because of Christ alone was not stated clearly anywhere on the watchlist this week — which is why the Core Lock standard, the threshold requiring that foundational claim to be explicit, was triggered on every single sermon. Until that sentence is preached on its own terms, the rest of the message floats. Warmth without foundation becomes pressure; encouragement without finished work becomes another assignment. The fix is not a softer tone or a kinder framing. It is a sentence — Christ, alone, finished — that the rest of the sermon is allowed to rest on.

The Index — How This Works

The Grace Clarity Index (GCI) measures how clearly a sermon articulates the finished work of Christ as the foundation of the believer's identity, security, and righteousness. It does not evaluate sincerity, salvation, or spiritual fruit. Each sermon is scored across 12 categories totaling 100 points.

Letter grades: A (90–100), B (80–89), C (65–79), D (50–64), F (0–49).

Two structural rules apply. The Ceiling Rule caps a sermon at 70 if the finished work is not clearly the foundation. The Core Lock Rule caps it at 60 if the believer's full righteousness before God because of Christ alone is not clearly stated. Both rules can apply; the lower cap is used.
