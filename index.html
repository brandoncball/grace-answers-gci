The Temperature

The aggregate landed at 9/100. Every sermon scored this week sat in the F band. The highest reading (39/100) belonged to a panel-format walkthrough of the pastoral epistles that name-checked the gospel without ever organizing the message around it. Last week's single C — the bright spot that pulled the average two points above the floor — was not repeated.

The Governing Pattern

The diagnostic question is not "Was Jesus mentioned?" It is "What was doing the theological work?" This week, in every scored sermon, the answer was the listener's response. The believer was on the clock in ten consecutive pulpits. One sermon framed Spirit-empowered breakthrough as a function of personal declaration. Another taught a marriage how-to that landed the listener on practical behavior changes. A third reframed an exposition on servant leadership into "this is what you must do to be an effective Christian leader." The cross showed up as story, not as ground.

Warm Words, Ungrounded

Several sermons used affectionate, encouraging, even tender language while leaving the finished work outside the load-bearing structure. Nearness without explanation is sentimentality, not theology. A listener could leave moved — even genuinely moved — without ever encountering a sentence stating that they are fully accepted by God right now because of what Christ already accomplished. The gospel was implied, gestured at, sometimes tacked on as a closing prayer. It was rarely the spine.

Where the Covenant Disappears

Old vs. New Covenant clarity landed at 1.6 / 10 — the lowest of the four headline metrics, and the lowest reading the index has logged on this category. Old Testament narratives were handed to New Covenant believers without translation. A Pentecost text became a manual for personal "supernatural therapy." A pre-resurrection encounter with a religious leader was converted into a present-tense Christian behavioral test. A passage about Christ's ransom was extracted for a leadership talk. If everything is for you, nothing is finished.

The Bright Spot

The week's highest scorer (39/100, F) was an honest verse-by-verse exposition that produced one of the cleanest substitutionary lines of the week — a clear statement that Christ became sin so that the believer could know the righteousness of God. The line is real. It is also surrounded by an entire message organized around what Christians must do to be effective leaders. That structure is what triggered the Core Lock standard — a threshold requiring that the foundational claim of the gospel be doing the theological work, not just appearing in the sermon. One strong line did not move the foundation.

Trend Context

This is the second consecutive week with the entire watchlist in the F band, and the third F-band week in the past four. The weekly average has held at 9/100 for two weeks running. The watchlist is not getting worse — it is settling at a baseline where finished-work clarity is the exception, not the structure.

When the cross is decoration and the listener is the driver, even a sincere sermon ends up asking the audience to finish what only Christ could finish.

The Editorial

The point of a sermon is not to bring people up to a standard. It is to declare that the standard has already been met for them. When that order gets reversed — when Christian living gets named before Christ's accomplished work gets named — the listener leaves with a weight they were never given to carry. The two-week pattern in this data is what that reversal looks like when it runs across an entire weekend of widely-watched pulpits. Not absent in language. Absent as the governing foundation.

The Index — How This Works

The Grace Clarity Index (GCI) measures how clearly a sermon articulates the finished work of Christ as the foundation of the believer's identity, security, and righteousness — and whether the apostolic indicative-imperative pattern is honored when fruit and calling come up. It does not evaluate sincerity or salvation. Each sermon is scored across 13 categories totaling 100 points. Grades: A 90–100, B 80–89, C 65–79, D 50–64, F 0–49. Two hard rules cap the result: the Ceiling Rule limits any sermon whose foundation is not the finished work to a maximum of 70, and the Core Lock Rule limits any sermon that does not explicitly state full righteousness in Christ alone to a maximum of 60.
