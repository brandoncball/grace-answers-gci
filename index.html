This week: tone was warm and sincere across the board, but what was doing the theological work in almost every pulpit was the listener's response — not the finished work of Christ.

The Patterns

The Temperature

A storm-warning week. Only one sermon of eight landed above the C line, and the majority clustered in the D-to-F range. What made the week remarkable was not what was missing — it was what was present. Warmth, intimacy, enthusiasm for God, real pastoral care. That was everywhere. What was doing the work of the sermon, though, was almost always the listener.

The Governing Pattern

In seven of the eight messages scored, the believer's response — speaking, declaring, activating, surrendering, meditating, engaging, re-engaging, holding on — carried the sermon from beginning to end. Christ was present as vocabulary in most of them. He was rarely present as the foundation. When the closing moment came, the listener was being asked to do something, feel something, or declare something, not to rest in something Christ had already done.

Warm but Ungrounded

This is the shape of the week. A sermon that opens with genuine compassion, tells a story the listener can feel, reaches for an encouraging scripture, and closes with an activation invitation. One sermon declared "he lived a perfect life, we are set free" — a strong gospel statement — and then immediately pivoted to a behavioral checklist covering marriage, parenthood, friendship, money, and sexuality. The gospel moment was real. It just did not govern. A few minutes later, the same listener was being asked to go all in, take off the veil, and refuse to identify with their flesh — the sermon had quietly shifted from a finished gift to an assignment.

The Bright Spot

One church this week let the finished work carry the message. The sermon opened on Ephesians 2:8–10 and framed the entire book as a study in union with Christ. A guest teacher walked through the distinctive Pauline idea that the believer is joined to Christ without being absorbed into him — that standing, life, and identity are grounded in Christ, not in effort. It honored the text. It handled the tension of distinct personhood inside union without flinching. The closing prayer rested on mercy and connection rather than activation. It did not hit an A because a thematic overview naturally does not carry every category, but it was the week's clearest signal that a popular-scale pulpit can preach the finished work as the foundation. It's a system feature — the Ceiling Rule and the Core Lock — that kept an otherwise strong sermon from climbing higher, not a criticism of what was said.

Where the Covenant Disappears

The Old-vs-New Covenant category averaged 4.1 out of 10. That is where this week's damage happens. When the Mosaic narrative is preached for principles rather than promise, when a prophecy spoken to exiled Israel is repackaged for personal breakthrough, when a wisdom book is preached as if it were moral instruction for the Christian — the covenant line blurs. Once the line blurs, the listener is living under the old arrangement regardless of what gets said about grace earlier in the sermon. Nearness without covenantal grounding is sentimentality, not theology.

When the believer's response governs the sermon, the finished work becomes decoration — present in vocabulary, absent in function.

The Editorial

None of these preachers are insincere. Several are genuinely gifted communicators. What the index is catching is a structural drift in American evangelical preaching: the finished work of Christ has become a doctrine pastors agree with more than a foundation they preach from. A week like this one is not a referendum on anyone's heart. It is a reminder that the New Covenant is not a posture we achieve — it is a reality we rest in. When that reality carries the sermon, the whole landscape changes.

The Index — How This Works

The Grace Clarity Index (GCI) measures how clearly a sermon articulates the finished work of Christ as the foundation of the believer's identity, security, and righteousness. It does not evaluate sincerity, salvation, or spiritual fruit. Each sermon is scored across 12 categories totaling 100 points.

Grade scale: A (90–100) · B (80–89) · C (65–79) · D (50–64) · F (0–49).
Ceiling Rule: When the finished work is not clearly the foundation, a sermon cannot exceed 70.
Core Lock Rule: When the believer's full righteousness in Christ alone is not clearly stated, a sermon cannot exceed 60.
