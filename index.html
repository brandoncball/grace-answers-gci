The Week's Headline

Nine widely-watched pulpits preached on the Sunday after Easter. The average landed at 54/100 (D, Overcast). Only one sermon reached the B range, one reached C, three sat in D, and four sat in F. That is a week in which finished-work theology is almost nowhere to be found. Easter had ended. The pulpits returned to their defaults.

The B-Range Outlier

The one sermon that crossed into Mostly Clear preached Daniel 3 — the fiery furnace and the 'fourth man' as pre-incarnate Christ. The theological instinct was strong: the preacher refused prosperity-faith with the line "even if he doesn't, I will continue to worship and honor him." That sentence alone lifted the sermon. What kept it from an A was the landing. The closing shifted into a full salvation invitation with repent-turn-surrender language — appropriate for the unbeliever, but for the gathered believer it re-centered the sermon on their act of turning rather than Christ's finished reception.

The Mental-Discipline Sermon

Two sermons this week built their applications on the believer's mental posture. Peace, in both cases, was framed as a discipline: "keep your mind fixed on Christ," "guard your inputs," "take every thought captive." One at least anchored positional peace in Romans 5:1 at the opening. The other buried its one finished-work sentence under an avalanche of "refix, refocus, re-engage" language. The pattern is the same: peace is treated as portable, losable, and maintained. In the New Covenant, peace is a verdict that stands whether or not the believer manages their mind.

"The covering was in the command."
The Submission Framework

Two sermons this week built on variations of the same structural move: submit more, and the blessing follows. The first framed it around "go under control, under authority, under cover" — making the believer's posture the hinge of every outcome. The second preached spiritual warfare as strategic sowing — the believer's life depends on discerning and deploying the right weapon for each battle. One line captured the whole system: "the covering was in the command." Under the new covenant, the covering is in Christ, not in obedience. These sermons do not say that. They reverse the arrow.

The Cultivated-Presence Pattern

One sermon opened with ten minutes of Romans 6 — "my job is to receive, not achieve, believe you are dead to sin" — and then spent forty minutes teaching the believer how to cultivate the experience. The explicit line was "physical obedience brings spiritual release." Tithing, baptism, raising hands were named as releases. That is sacramental transactionalism wrapped in charismatic vocabulary. If the believer cannot produce the breakthrough, the implied diagnosis is insufficient hunger or insufficient physical obedience. The opening Romans 6 theology was real. It just was not the engine.

The Activation Sermon

Another sermon slot this week was filled with what was effectively a volunteer recruitment talk. A "5 Faith Catalysts" framework treated faith as a muscle that must be activated through engagement — and the closing was a literal QR-code invitation to serve in kids ministry. The theological claim — that engagement activates faith — is the opposite of biblical faith. Biblical faith is trust in something outside the self, specifically in Christ's completed work. Muscle-faith cannot produce rest, because it has made the believer's own spiritual output the hinge.

The Law-Heavy Rally

One sermon closed the weakest tier with a 'stand for truth' rally built on Pilate's "What is truth?" question. The one sharp moment — the "love is God vs. God is love — same words, different order, different world" distinction — would have been enough to build a whole sermon on. Instead the sermon landed on the believer's vigilance: "it's time we start getting loud about salvation." When a sermon's closing move is about the believer holding the line, the cross has gone missing.

The Week in One Sentence

Eight of the nine sermons this week preached grace-adjacent, performance-driven theology. Only one broke clear of it, and even that one retreated at the close. The landing — the final five minutes — is still where widely-viewed pulpits default to activation. Until that default changes, every Sunday will look like this one.

The Index — How This Works

The Grace Clarity Index scores sermons across 12 categories designed to measure whether the finished work of Christ is doing the theological work in the message. The index asks: Is identity rooted in Christ's completed redemption, or is it set up as a runway to what you must do? Is assurance grounded in "It is finished," or in what you must maintain? Is transformation portrayed as the Spirit's work in union with Christ, or as the result of your effort and response?

Each sermon receives a composite score (0–100), placed on a weather scale. The scale reflects not how good the preaching is, but whether the sermon's theological center holds to New Covenant clarity. Public report includes aggregate data only.
